A letter for your 45th

I say a lot of things, Papa, but I don't say this enough. So I built it a website.

Dear Papa,

Yup, today is the big day — you turn 45! — and I've been trying to figure out what you give the man who has already given you everything. I couldn't think of buying you anything, so I made you this instead — a whole website, only for you, because a card was never going to be big enough for what I have to say, and honestly, even the internet might not be.

Forty-five years ago, on 19 July 1981, the world got you. It took the world a while to make you and Mom meet and get me — 28 more years, to 8 December 2009, 9:00 PM — and from that exact moment you have been the best father in the world every single second. Every minute, every hour, every day, every month, every year. I've done the maths — that's over 520 MILLION seconds of you being there for me, and I genuinely can't remember one single second where you didn't. Not one. Think about that. In sixteen and a half years you have never once been too tired, too busy, or too stressed to be my father — even on the days I now realize you had every reason to be all three.

While making this website I went through the photos, and Papa, I had to stop halfway because it hit me all at once. There's a picture of you holding me in the water while I kiss a dolphin, and you're not even looking at the dolphin — you're smiling at me. There's one where I'm covering your eyes on the beach and we're both laughing at absolutely nothing. There's Mom, you and me holding hands in the waves. There's us on a plane throwing peace signs like two kids, because with you even a flight feels like the destination. There's you handing me a Ferrari key like it was the most normal thing in the world. And there's us now, standing next to cars at midnight, doing the exact same things we did when I was three — just taller. Sixteen years of photos, Papa, and in every single one of them you're either holding me, watching me, or making sure I'm having the best day of my life.

People throw around words like hero and role model. I don't. When I say you're my hero, I mean that when I imagine the man I want to become, I'm just imagining you. You are my pillar of strength, my motivational speaker, my teacher, my guide, my safe place and my best friend, all in one person — and somehow that same person also finds time to work harder than anyone I have ever seen in my life. I watch you, Papa. You think nobody notices the long days, the late nights, the phone calls at dinner, the pressure you carry without ever letting it reach your face. I notice everything. I see how you walk out of the house every morning carrying all of our dreams on your shoulders, and how you walk back in every night still smiling, like the weight is nothing, like it's a privilege.

Everything I have, you built. The school I go to — you put me there. The holidays I remember — you made them happen. The dreams I'm allowed to have — you paid for the right to dream them. The confidence I walk around with, like a boy who knows that whatever happens, wherever he falls, his father is standing behind him — that confidence is the most expensive thing you have ever given me, and you gave it to me for free, every single day, just by being you. I have never once in my life felt afraid of the future. Not because I'm brave, but because I have you.

So here's my promise on your 45th birthday, and I want it in writing so that you can hold me to it: I will work as hard as you have worked and push myself to work even harder. I'm going to keep learning, keep improving, keep pushing, keep going on the days I want to give up — because on those days I think of you, and I remind myself that if Dad can carry everything he carries and still try to show up every single morning with a smile, then I have no excuse. And I think everything good in me — the ambition, the discipline that I'm trying to build, the way I treat people, the fact that I even know what hard work looks like — all of it comes from watching you. And one day I hope you can look at me and feel even a little bit of pride, and be proud of me as your son.

And here's my wish, and I mean it more than any wish I will ever make, even on my own birthday: I hope 45 is the year life starts paying you back. You have spent decades making everyone else's dreams come true — mine, Mom's. Every dream of your own that you've parked so ours could keep running — I hope this is the year you get to take them out of the garage and drive out every single one of them. And I hope you know that whatever I achieve in my life, wherever I end up, a part of every single win will always be yours, just as much as it is mine.

Happy 45th birthday, Dad. Thank you for every lesson, every sacrifice, every hug, every piece of advice, every opportunity, every laugh, every memory, every single moment. I love you today, I loved you yesterday, I'll love you tomorrow, and I will love you for the rest of my life — and knowing you, you'll still find a way to love me more. It's the only competition I will ever lose.

Your proud son,
Medhansh
